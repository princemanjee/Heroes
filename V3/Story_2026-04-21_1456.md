## Response

**Title:** _The Last Honest Check in Bay City_

**Story:**

The rain had been falling on Bay City for six days, which was three days past interesting and four days past our laundry.

I was watching it through the slats of our office blinds when the buzzer went. Jamie was at her desk, tapping at a laptop with the concentration of a woman trying to land an airplane on a postage stamp. From my angle, she took up most of the office; at six-foot-two, Jamie takes up most of anywhere she goes, and standing at five-foot-nine with prescription lenses, I've made peace with being the short half of Manjee & Carver, Private Investigations.

"Client," she said, not looking up.

"At two in the morning?"

"On our website. Took an appointment. Booked it eleven minutes ago."

"People who book appointments at one-forty-nine AM are usually in one of three categories," I said. "Drunks, liars, or grieving. Usually at least two of three."

The buzzer went again, longer.

"And the rich," Jamie added. "Category four. The ones who assume that if they're awake, you should be awake too."

---

Celeste Varga was category four with a category two finish and a wet category three coat. She sat in our client chair like it had offended her, wringing rain from the hem of grey cashmere that probably cost more than our rent. Mid-forties. Good skin. Hands that hadn't done manual work in twenty years but were doing the work of seeming worried very, very hard.

"My brother is missing," she said.

"Since when?"

"Three days."

"Police?"

"He's a grown man. A private man. He'd hate that."

Jamie set a cup of coffee in front of her — the kind Jamie makes, which is to say the kind that tastes like a polite apology. Celeste ignored it.

"His name is Alaric Varga. He runs Varga Systems. Green-energy logistics. You may have heard —"

"Helicopter pad on the roof of the Pacific Building," I said. "Private tower, no listed number, his face on the cover of every magazine that doesn't have my face on it. Which is all of them."

"Yes."

"Why us?"

Celeste lifted her chin. "Because you're discreet."

"We're cheap," I said.

"Both," Jamie said pleasantly.

She named a retainer. It was, in fact, the kind of number that made Jamie's pen stop over her notebook and hang there like it had hit a force field. I watched Celeste's face while she said it. I watched her eyes, which did not change, and her fingers, which did. Her left index finger tapped twice against her cashmere knee — once while she said the number, and once just after.

She was lying about something. I didn't know what yet. But the tell was in the second tap.

"We'll find him," I said.

---

The Pacific Building was a forty-story mistake made of glass and hubris. The helipad on its roof was deserted at three AM, the Sikorsky tied down against the wind, its rotors ticking in the rain. I'd gotten my pilot's license for a case two years ago and never stopped — people underestimate how often a helicopter solves your problems, and Alaric Varga had the best-maintained bird in the city. I wanted a look at it.

"You realize," Jamie said, as we picked the service-door lock, "that when a client describes her brother as a 'private man,' what she usually means is —"

"He's hiding from her. Yeah."

"So we're not looking for him. We're chasing him."

"Nobody said detective work was honest."

The penthouse was clean. Too clean. Jamie went for the office. I went for the bedroom, because bedrooms tell you what offices lie about. The bed was made with the fussy precision of a man who lived alone and liked it that way. On the nightstand, a half-read novel about a man faking his death in Belize. I closed it, slid it back, and noticed the dust.

"Jamie."

"Mm."

"This bed hasn't been slept in for a week. At least."

"Laptop," she said, walking back in with a slim silver thing under her arm. "Password is his birthday run backwards. Nobody teaches the rich anything."

She set it on the bed and opened a shell. Numbers ran. Numbers kept running. Numbers stopped and she stood very still.

"Prince."

"What."

"Celeste didn't lose her brother. She laundered through him. Forty million a year in offshore shell returns routed through Varga Systems' green-energy transit contracts. About six months ago, Alaric started pulling the threads."

"And hired her."

"And hired us. To find him before he can talk."

Which was, of course, when the door downstairs opened.

---

The man who came up the service stairs had the broad-shouldered, pressed-collar silhouette of a guy paid very well to do one specific thing. He had a suppressor on his pistol. He also had bad hearing, because Jamie — contemplative, soft-footed, slower-than-you-think Jamie — stepped out of the bathroom doorway as he passed and hit him across the temple with the laptop like she'd been practicing for a year.

The man went sideways. His gun went off into the floor. My shoulder went off too, a second later, when he rolled and fired up at me from one knee — a hot, shocking, _tugging_ pain that knocked me into the doorjamb and made the room list ten degrees to port.

"Prince —"

"Helipad. Go."

I kicked the gun out of his reach, or tried to; my kicking leg didn't have its usual conviction. Jamie grabbed my good arm and pulled me up the stairs two at a time, her long legs eating the distance, me scrambling to keep pace. Above us the storm was biblical. Below us the shooter was shouting into a radio.

"Can you fly it?" Jamie said.

"With one arm?"

"Prince —"

"Yes. Probably. Mostly."

She got me strapped in. I got the rotors up. The shooter came out onto the roof with a friend, both of them opening up; a round went through the windshield and took a chip out of my glasses. I pulled collective and the Sikorsky lifted off the pad like a drunk apologizing to a lamppost — heavy, sideways, _alive_ — and we were out over the bay before either of us breathed.

"You've been shot," Jamie said, very calmly.

"I noticed."

"You're bleeding on the yoke."

"I'll buff it."

"Prince."

"Jamie. We're alive. Alaric is alive somewhere. His sister is the thing he's hiding from. And we have her check in the drawer."

She looked at me, rain beading on the windshield between us. Her blue-grey eyes went to the envelope-shaped bulge in my coat pocket.

"That retainer," she said, "is four months of rent."

"I know."

"We could take it and leave. Tell her we couldn't find him. She goes back to her life. Alaric stays gone. Nobody else shoots you in the shoulder."

"We could."

The helicopter's instruments ticked. The rain drummed. Bay City's neon bled upward through the clouds beneath us — red and blue and the particular rotten green of money.

"But we won't," Jamie said.

"No."

"Because you're congenitally incapable of cashing a check from someone who tried to have us shot."

"And because you already know where he is."

She smiled, which is a rare weather event. "Pier 19. The laptop had coordinates. He's on his own boat."

"See?" I said. "We're detectives."

---

Alaric Varga was a smaller man than his magazine covers suggested, and he was on the foredeck of a thirty-foot sailboat with a shotgun, looking like a man who'd been waiting three days to stop being waiting.

I landed the Sikorsky on the pier — badly, but landed is landed — and Jamie got out first because she was the one not bleeding. I watched her unfold up out of the passenger seat, tall against the rain, and jog the length of the pier with her hands out and empty.

"Mr. Varga. Your sister hired us. We know. We came to get you out."

He lowered the shotgun half an inch.

Which was when Celeste's other guy came around the container stack at the end of the pier with his own rifle up, and fired.

Here's what they don't tell you about getting shot the second time in one night: you're not ready for it, and you're also _more_ ready for it, and somewhere in the middle of those two truths is where Jamie moved. She didn't analyze. She didn't think. She pivoted, dropped her shoulder into Alaric Varga's chest, and put him over the side of the boat into eight feet of harbor water — and the rifle round that had been meant for the back of his head went through the fiberglass rail instead.

Then she was falling too, because the rifleman's second shot had found her thigh, and the deck, and the rain, and all of it at once.

I was already moving. The shooter hadn't expected me to have a pistol in my left hand, because nobody expects the short bespectacled one to be ambidextrous, which is a thing you learn to lean on in this line of work. Two rounds. One hit. He dropped.

I dragged Jamie off the deck. I pulled Alaric out of the harbor. I got both of them into the helicopter, in that order, because Jamie would have yelled at me if I did it any other way.

---

Alaric's wife, Mira, met us at St. Vincent's. She was seven months pregnant and crying before the automatic doors finished opening. Alaric walked in under his own power, soaked, alive, his hand over hers on her belly before he'd even finished saying her name. I stood in the hallway with rain still dripping out of my coat cuffs, watching it happen.

Jamie was in surgery. The round had missed the femoral by a centimeter and a half, which is to say — by the width of Jamie's instinct.

Celeste Varga had vanished. She would reappear three months later in a jurisdiction with no extradition, which is a story for another rainy night.

When Jamie woke up, I was in the chair next to her bed with the retainer envelope in my lap. I tore it in half while she watched.

"So we're broke again," she said. Her voice was sandpapered from the tube.

"So we're right again."

"Prince. My thigh _hurts_."

"I'll get you coffee."

"Your coffee is worse than the bullet."

"You say the sweetest things."

Outside, the rain was still coming down on Bay City, and somewhere in a bright apartment a child was about to be born to parents who were both there to meet him — which is not a thing noir usually lets you have. I figured we'd earned it once. I wasn't sure we'd earn it twice.

I taped the torn half of the check to our office door the next morning, right under the stenciled letters that read MANJEE & CARVER, which are in that order for reasons I maintain are purely alphabetical.

Jamie, when she got out of the hospital two weeks later, taped the other half under hers.
